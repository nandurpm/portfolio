How I Think About Engineering Design Reviews

May 28, 2026 4 min read Engineering

Design reviews are moments where engineering discipline meets practical reality. I approach them as systematic checkpoints, not just compliance boxes.

The Core Questions

Every review should answer: Does the design solve the actual problem? Can it be manufactured reliably? Will it survive real-world use? What happens when someone makes a mistake during assembly or maintenance?

The Checklist Framework

I use a practical checklist organized around design intent, manufacturability, quality risk, and field reliability. This structure keeps reviews focused and traceable.

Design Intent

Verify the design actually addresses the original requirement. This seems obvious, but feature creep and missed specifications happen more often than you'd think.

Manufacturability

Review with the people who will actually make it. Tool accessibility, fixture clearance, assembly sequence—these details either make manufacturing smooth or create shortcuts that damage quality.

Quality Risk

Identify failure modes that would escape normal inspection. Where are the weak points? What warnings or instructions prevent assembly errors?

Field Reliability

Consider maintenance, repair, and replacement scenarios. A design that's impossible to service properly is incomplete, even if it works initially.

Making It Stick

The review is only valuable if the findings get built into production documentation. Every issue noted becomes either a design change, an inspection step, or a work instruction.

Clear documentation from design review becomes institutional memory that prevents the same mistakes from repeating.
