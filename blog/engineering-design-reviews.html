How I Think About Engineering Design Reviews

May 28, 20268 min readEngineering

A design review is not a ceremonial meeting held only because a procedure requires one. It is the point where an idea is challenged before it becomes an expensive drawing revision, a production delay, a field complaint, or a safety risk. A useful review does not ask only whether the design looks complete. It asks whether the design can be built, tested, inspected, installed, maintained, and understood by people who were not part of the original discussion.

I prefer a review process that is structured enough to prevent omissions but practical enough to support real engineering work. The objective is not to create the longest checklist. The objective is to expose uncertainty while changes are still cheap.

Start with the actual requirement

The first question is simple: what problem is this design expected to solve? A drawing can be technically neat and still solve the wrong problem. Before reviewing dimensions, wiring, materials, or software logic, the team should confirm the operating requirement, interfaces, environmental conditions, acceptance criteria, and constraints.

Requirements should be written in a way that can later be verified. “The unit should be reliable” is not testable. “The connector shall remain secure after the defined vibration and pull test” is testable. Every important requirement should have a clear method of verification: inspection, calculation, measurement, simulation, or physical test.

Review the complete system, not isolated parts

Many failures occur at interfaces. A mechanical part may be correct by itself, an electrical drawing may be correct by itself, and the assembly can still fail because the two were never reviewed together. A system review should examine power, signal, mounting, routing, accessibility, heat, movement, environmental exposure, tolerances, and service access.

  • Are connector locations accessible during assembly and service?
  • Can wiring be routed without sharp bends, rubbing, tension, or accidental contact with moving parts?
  • Are labels and terminal references consistent across drawings, BOMs, and test documents?
  • Can dimensional variation create interference or reduce required clearance?
  • Does one failure create another unsafe or difficult-to-diagnose condition?

Check manufacturability and assembly sequence

A design is incomplete when production can build it only by improvising. The review should consider the order of assembly, tool access, fixture requirements, handling risks, inspection visibility, and the possibility of human error. A component that can be installed in the wrong orientation needs a prevention method, not only an instruction telling the operator to be careful.

Good design reduces dependence on memory. Clear markings, keyed connectors, controlled wire lengths, accessible fasteners, standardized hardware, and logical routing reduce variation. The best engineering change is often one that removes a possible mistake instead of adding another inspection step.

Identify quality risks before release

Quality checks must be linked to realistic failure modes. A review should ask what can go wrong, how the failure can be detected, and whether the detection happens before the product reaches the next stage. Visual inspection alone is weak for faults that are hidden after assembly. Critical electrical connections may require continuity checks, torque confirmation, insulation tests, or controlled functional testing.

A review finding is useful only when it becomes a design change, a verified control, or an assigned action with a clear owner and completion date.

Make drawings and documents agree

Release documents form one system. Drawings, wiring schedules, BOMs, part numbers, labels, software versions, work instructions, test sheets, and revision records must tell the same story. A mismatch between two documents creates uncertainty on the shop floor, and uncertainty is normally resolved by assumption.

I check reference designations, terminal numbers, wire sizes, lengths, colours, connector names, revision levels, quantities, and notes across related documents. Repeated information should preferably come from one controlled source or formula rather than being typed separately in several locations.

Consider testing and fault diagnosis

A design should make verification possible. Test points, status indications, accessible terminals, diagnostic messages, and clear signal references reduce troubleshooting time. During review, it is worth asking how a technician will distinguish between a power fault, wiring fault, sensor fault, configuration issue, and controller fault.

Testing should cover normal operation, boundary conditions, incorrect inputs, loss of supply, disconnected devices, and recovery after a fault. The aim is not merely to demonstrate that the system works once. The aim is to understand how it behaves when conditions are imperfect.

Close the review properly

A meeting without action control is only a discussion. Each issue should be recorded with its impact, owner, target date, and verification method. Major findings should be closed with evidence: an updated drawing, calculation, test result, photo, inspection record, or approved technical decision.

The review is complete when the released design is consistent, buildable, testable, traceable, and suitable for its intended use. That standard is more demanding than “no one found another comment,” but it is also what prevents repeated problems later.
